## January Writing into the Dark Online Workshop

Writing into the Dark is a six-week online workshop designed to help fiction writers break free from the myths of outlining and rewriting. The approach teaches writers to produce clean, finished first-draft copy by engaging the creative voice directly — the same way a reader experiences a story. Each week covers a distinct phase of the novel-writing process, from openings through endings, with practical techniques for getting unstuck and staying in forward motion.

### Introduction

The workshop operates on a simple weekly structure. New sessions appear each Tuesday. Once live, all content remains permanently accessible for review at any point in the future.

Assignments are not mandatory, but completing them and submitting for feedback dramatically increases the value of the workshop. Deadlines matter: assignments submitted by the stated due date receive individual written responses. Participants who miss the window should still complete the work for their own benefit.

**Submissions go as email attachments.** The subject line must match exactly what is specified below each assignment. Spam filters are strict — an incorrect subject line means the email may never be read.

The sixth week carries no assignment beyond reviewing everything covered in weeks one through five. This is by design: the final week is synthesis and integration, not new material.

For questions of any kind, email with "workshops" in the subject line to get through spam filters.

### Exercise / Action Items

- Set up email with the correct subject line format before submitting any assignment.
- Treat the weekly release schedule as a reading commitment, not a deadline for completion.
- Plan to review Week 2 content two or three times — it is denser and more provocative than the others.

### Key Takeaways

- Content stays available indefinitely once unlocked. Return to any session as needed.
- Assignments create accountability and generate personalized feedback.
- The sixth week intentionally has no new assignment — use it to consolidate.


## Week 1: What Is Writing into the Dark?

Week one lays the foundational principles of the method: what it is, why it works, what makes it feel the way it does, and what beliefs writers must discard before the technique becomes accessible. This week covers the definition of writing into the dark, the central role of fun, the two-voice model of the writer's mind, the myths that block creative work, the feeling of writing without a map, and the structure of sticking points throughout the novel.

### Defining Writing into the Dark

**Writing into the dark** means writing without an outline, without character sketches, without any preliminary preparation. The writer simply begins typing.

This is not recklessness. It is a deliberate mirror of the reading process. When a reader opens a novel, they encounter character, setting, and situation in sequence — not a summary of what is about to happen. Writing into the dark replicates that sequential discovery for the writer. The writer tells themselves a story exactly as they would read one.

This is also why the method is counterintuitive. English-language education frames fiction as something engineered from a blueprint. Students learn to analyze finished novels by breaking them apart — which gives the impression that authors had all the pieces sorted out before writing a single word. They did not. The analytical lens of the classroom creates a false picture of how stories are made.

Most long-term professional fiction writers work without outlines. The method is not uncommon — it is simply underrepresented in public discourse because writers who work this way rarely write books about it.

### Fun: The Non-Negotiable Element

Fun is the most frequently repeated word in this workshop. It is not motivational fluff.

If writing is painful, dull, or feels like a Herculean task, something is wrong with the process — not the writer's talent or willpower. Writers are entertainers. If the entertainer is not entertained, that quality of dread bleeds through every sentence and reaches the reader.

Most writers started out telling stories in childhood for the pure pleasure of it. Somewhere along the way, the word "work" got attached to writing. That single word is one of the most destructive forces in a long-term writing career.

**The prescription:** Stop calling writing "work." Never let the word attach to the process of making fiction. Write what you are passionate about. If a project sounds boring before you begin, decline it. If a project sounds fun, begin immediately.

Professional longevity runs on this principle. The writers who are still producing at high volume after twenty, thirty, or forty years are doing it because they genuinely enjoy it. The ones who burned out or disappeared were grinding through something that felt like obligation.

### The Two-Voice Model

Every writer operates with two competing mental processes:

1. **The critical voice** — the analytical, fear-based, pattern-matching system that sits at the front of conscious thought. Its origin is protective: it helps humans identify and avoid danger. Its only job in creative work, however, is to stop the writer. Everything the critical voice says — "that's stupid," "this is a waste of time," "you don't know where this is going" — is a protective reflex, not useful feedback.

2. **The creative voice** — the imaginative, story-building system that operates below conscious deliberation. Think of a two-year-old who has been listening to stories since birth. The creative voice has absorbed structure, character, emotion, pacing, and genre expectation through thousands of hours of reading and being read to. It knows what a story is. It does not need instruction from the critical voice. It needs permission and space to play.

Writing into the dark is the practice of giving the creative voice space. The critical voice is not conquered — it surfaces constantly. But the practice is learning to recognize it when it speaks and to not obey it.

### Myths That Stop Writers

All myths in fiction writing trace back to the critical voice. The most persistent ones include:

- **The rewriting myth:** That first drafts are always garbage and that real writing is rewriting. Most long-term professional writers produce clean, complete first drafts and never rewrite them. Developmental editing, in particular, is a financial and creative trap.
- **The outline myth:** That a writer needs an outline before beginning. Outlining produces safe, predictable, uninspired work because it originates entirely from the critical voice.
- **The character-sketch myth:** That a writer must know their character before introducing them. Characters reveal themselves through the act of writing.
- **The golden-word myth:** The belief that every word written is precious and must be protected. This belief freezes the creative voice and kills forward motion.
- **The muse myth:** That story ideas arrive from some external source, and a writer must wait for inspiration. Ideas are not rare. The ability to generate them is a muscle, trained by writing continuously.

The waste-of-time objection deserves specific attention. Many writers resist writing without a plan because they fear wasting words. Consider what is actually wasted: hours spent on outlines, character sketches, worldbuilding documents, and research that precedes a single word of actual fiction. That is the real time loss. Writing always produces something — at minimum, practice. Outlining produces nothing a reader will ever read.

### What It Feels Like

Writing into the dark is not a gentle, serene experience. It is a roller coaster.

The opening climb is exciting. The writer establishes a character in a setting, gets into the voice, and the story begins to take shape. Then the first drop: the writer does not know what happens next. The track disappears. The critical voice surges in with warnings and questions.

For writers who have trained themselves to trust the creative voice — the two-year-old at the controls — the experience is thrilling. The story goes in directions that couldn't have been planned. Twists appear that surprise the writer as much as any reader. The book becomes something the writer genuinely wants to read, because they genuinely do not know where it is going.

For writers still controlled by the critical voice, the drops feel like failure. The not-knowing feels like incompetence rather than storytelling.

The roller coaster lands. The story ends. And then, because the ride was genuinely exciting, the instinct is to buy another ticket.

### Glitch Points and the Novel's Architecture

Every novel, whether outlined or written into the dark, has predictable points of difficulty. For writers working in the dark, these are called **glitch points**:

1. **The opening** — the largest and most fear-laden glitch point. Starting at all requires suppressing the critical voice's objections to beginning without a plan.
2. **Pages 50–100** — confidence from the opening drains away. The story looks like nothing. This is where the third point of the novel's structure arrives, and where most writers stop.
3. **The approach to the climax** — fear intensifies as the resolution looms. Who did it? How does this end? What if I wrote myself into a corner?
4. **The ending and validation** — the climax is not the ending. After the climax, the writer must remain in the story through the validation phase.

The workshop is structured around these four glitch points. Each week targets a specific phase and provides concrete techniques for moving through it.

### Exercise / Action Items

**Assignment 1, Part 1:** List five myths about outlining and rewriting that you were taught. Try to remember who taught them (a teacher, a workshop, a book on craft, a writer friend). If you cannot remember the source, just list the myths.

**Assignment 1, Part 2:** Write a 500–800-word opening to a fiction piece set in the present day, involving the recent solar eclipse. No fantasy, no science fiction, no historical fiction. Write immediately — do not plan, do not outline, do not sit with it for two days. Write the opening the moment you finish reading the assignment.

Depth is the primary criterion for evaluation. The character must come alive on the page through sensory detail and emotional specificity, not through description alone.

### Key Takeaways

- Writing into the dark mirrors the reading process. The writer discovers the story in the same sequence the reader will.
- Fun is not optional. It is the engine of a long-term career.
- The critical voice exists to protect — not to create. Its job, in writing, is to stop you. Recognize it and refuse to obey.
- All major myths about fiction writing (outlines, rewrites, character sketches) originate in the critical voice.
- The feeling of not knowing what happens next is not a problem. It is the condition that produces compelling fiction.
- Glitch points are predictable. Knowing they are coming makes them survivable.


## Week 2: Openings — Getting Started Without an Idea

Week two focuses entirely on the opening phase of a novel or story. This is the most fear-saturated moment in the writing-into-the-dark method. Week two addresses the beliefs that block writers before the first word, the practical triggers that get a story started, the role of depth in establishing character, the place of attitude and voice, and how to use cycling to build and sustain momentum in an opening.

### The Special Snowflake Problem

Before any technique can work, a belief system must be examined: the idea that a writer's words must be perfect before they are committed to the page.

**The special snowflake** is a useful label for this belief. It captures the implicit position: that you are different from every other writer, that your words carry exceptional weight, that imperfection is a catastrophe. This belief is widespread among beginning and intermediate writers and is entirely counterproductive.

No professional writer's words are perfect. The standard measure of craft is not perfection — it is the best work achievable at this particular moment, with this particular skill level, on this particular story. That standard improves with every book written. The writer who refuses to commit imperfect words to the page simply does not improve.

The antidote to the special snowflake problem is a direct, unflinching look at the position and its implications: to believe words must be perfect is to believe you are more skilled than every long-term professional writer who produces imperfect, finished, published work continuously. That belief does not survive examination.

Write imperfect sentences. Release them. Write the next one.

### The Idea Problem and the Myth of the Muse

Waiting for an idea before beginning is one of the most reliable ways to stop writing indefinitely.

The **muse** — the concept of inspiration arriving from outside the writer — is a fantasy. It is a useful fantasy if it makes the creative experience feel magical, but it becomes destructive the moment a writer uses it as a prerequisite. "I'll start when I have an idea" translates directly to "I'll start never."

Ideas are generated by writing, not before it. The muscle that produces story ideas is trained through the act of writing stories. It is not trained by waiting, planning, or notebook-keeping.

Long-term professional writers do not write down ideas. If an idea is worth pursuing, it resurfaces when they sit down to write. If it does not resurface, it was not strong enough to justify the time. The test is simple: can the writer remember the concept three or four days later, when they sit down at the computer? If yes, use it. If not, it was not worth the page.

This applies especially to the opening of a writing session. The writer does not need an idea before sitting down. The writer needs a trigger.

### Triggers: How to Begin Without a Plan

A **trigger** is the smallest unit of story possibility — just enough to put fingers on keys and begin. The three primary triggers are:

1. **Character** — A person in a situation. Age, mood, physical detail, something slightly unusual about them.
2. **Setting** — A specific place. Not a genre backdrop but an actual location with sensory texture.
3. **Problem** — Occasionally, a compelling situation arrives fully formed and can serve as the opening hook. This is the rarest of the three triggers. Do not wait for it.

The seven-point structure of Western fiction begins with character in a setting. That is not a formula — it is an observation about how stories work. Readers always read for character. They cannot connect to a setting without a human consciousness experiencing it. They cannot care about a plot without a character to inhabit it.

One practical trigger technique: **half titles.** Open a table of contents from any older magazine or anthology. Pick half of a title at random — not the full title, just one piece of it. Combine two halves from different sources. Example: "The Cure" from one title plus "Sleeping Woman" from another becomes "The Cure for the Sleeping Woman." Type that title. Then start writing.

The title does not matter. It is a key that starts the engine. The story that emerges will be whatever the creative voice produces, not whatever the title implies.

Other triggers work equally well: the name of a restaurant, a character walking down a street dressed in the wrong clothing for the weather, a sound heard through a closed door. The trigger is not the story. It is just the doorway.

### Depth: Getting the Reader Into the Character

**Depth** is the technique of grounding readers inside a character's subjective experience of a setting — through the character's senses, opinions, emotions, and specific observations.

It is the difference between:

- "The diner was busy." (No depth — external observation, reader stays outside)
- "The smell hit her first: bacon grease and burnt coffee, the same smell as her grandmother's kitchen in Bakersfield, which was where she absolutely did not want to be thinking about right now." (Depth — reader is inside the character's sensory and emotional experience)

Depth is not description. Description is what the camera sees. Depth is what the character feels, notices, interprets, and reacts to.

When writing into the dark, depth is the writer's first tool. Before the plot arrives — before anything interesting "happens" — there is a character in a specific place, and the writer's job is to make that person real to the reader. Depth does the work.

Depth also makes openings easier to write. When the writer focuses on getting into the character's sensory experience of a setting, the critical voice has less room to operate. The writer is not trying to be clever or construct a plot — just trying to see what the character sees.

### Attitude and Voice

The writing voice of the instructor in these sessions is deliberately provocative. That is instructive.

**Attitude** is how a writer's personality and belief system come through the words. It is not something applied after the fact — it is present in the syntax, the word choices, the things a character notices and ignores.

Many writers, especially early in their careers, attempt to suppress their own personality in their prose. They write what they think a serious literary work should sound like. The result is flat, generic, interchangeable fiction with no distinguishing voice.

When writing into the dark, if a character's attitude starts coming through strongly — if the character starts saying things the writer didn't plan, pushing back against the situation, refusing to behave according to the writer's initial instincts — that is the creative voice operating at full speed. Follow it. Do not pull the character back to behave. Do not silence the attitude.

Voice is not style. Voice is the combination of who the writer is and who the character is in collision. When that collision produces something surprising, unexpected, or uncomfortable, the story is working.

### Cycling: Building Momentum From the Start

**Cycling** is the core technical practice of writing into the dark. It is not rewriting. It is a momentum-building technique that produces clean, finished prose in a single pass through the story.

The process works like this:

1. Begin writing. Move forward for 200–400 words.
2. Stop and jump out of the story.
3. Go back to the beginning — word one — and read forward again.
4. While reading, make only **positive, additive changes**: add a detail, clarify a phrase, deepen a sensory moment. Never cut, never criticize.
5. When momentum returns and the stopping point is reached, power through it and write new material.
6. Repeat.

Each cycle is slightly longer than the last. The story becomes richer and the writer's confidence builds. By the time the opening is complete, it has been read four or five times and is genuinely clean — not because it was corrected after the fact, but because it was built clean through accumulation.

**The critical rule for cycling:** only positive thoughts. If the inner voice says "that's bad," ignore it and keep moving. The critical voice uses that thought as a foothold. The moment the writer agrees that something is bad, the brake is engaged. If a sentence sounds wrong, cycle past it — the creative voice will often fix it on the next pass without being explicitly told to.

### Why the First Four Sessions Matter

The attitude displayed across the first four sessions of this week is deliberate. It is designed to surface the beliefs that stop writers before they start.

If a session triggered discomfort, that discomfort identifies a belief worth examining. The special snowflake belief. The golden-word belief. The idea-as-prerequisite belief. These are not trivial opinions — they are the mechanisms by which the critical voice enforces its agenda of stopping the writer.

The writer who still believes, after week two, that their words must be perfect cannot write into the dark. The creative voice cannot operate under that kind of pressure. The practice requires imperfect words written forward, fast, and with genuine curiosity about what comes next.

### Exercise / Action Items

**Assignment 2:** Write a 500–800-word opening to a short story or novel set in the present day. The setting: a restaurant called "The Lost Face Restaurant." The type of restaurant should match the last restaurant you personally visited. (If you went to a Thai restaurant last week, The Lost Face Restaurant is a Thai restaurant.)

Read the assignment once. If an opening comes immediately, begin typing. If nothing comes immediately, begin typing anyway. Do not think about it for two days and then submit. If you did that, do not submit it — the exercise requires immediate commitment.

Depth is the criterion. Cycling is the method.

### Key Takeaways

- The "special snowflake" belief — that words must be perfect — destroys the creative voice's ability to function. Releasing imperfect words is the practice.
- Ideas come from writing, not before it. Do not wait for inspiration. Begin with a trigger.
- The three triggers for openings are character, setting, and problem. Character and setting are reliable. Problem is occasional.
- Depth is the technique that pulls readers into a story. It is the character's sensory and emotional experience of a setting, not external description.
- Attitude is voice in action. When a character pushes back with unexpected personality, follow it.
- Cycling builds momentum and produces clean copy without a second draft. All changes during cycling are additive and positive only.
- The critical voice will speak. Do not obey it.


## Week 3: Writing Clean — Cycling, Time, and Warning Signs

Week three shifts from openings to the mechanics of sustaining forward motion through the body of a novel. The central topics are: the ultimate goal of the method, why writing sloppy is destructive, how cycling developed and how to practice it, the warning signs that indicate the critical voice has seized control, and how time constraints shape the writer's relationship to the work.

### The Ultimate Goal: One Draft, Finished, Done

The goal of writing into the dark is a complete, clean first draft that never requires a second draft.

This is not a theoretical aspiration. Long-term professional writers — those with decades of production behind them — almost universally work this way. They write a book. They get to the end. The book is done. A copy editor may catch typos. A first reader may flag a continuity error. But the story itself is not revised, restructured, or rewritten.

The reason is simple: looking at the work again produces diminishing returns. Once a book is written, the writer has read it. Reading it again — to "improve" it — is the equivalent of rereading a novel you just finished. Why would you do that? The story is already known. The energy that would go into revision is better spent on the next book.

This is not a shortcut. It requires writing clean in the first pass. Cycling is what makes that possible.

### Why Writing Sloppy Is Destructive

The advice to "just get it down" — to write quickly and carelessly on the assumption that a later draft will fix everything — is one of the most common and most damaging instructions given to fiction writers.

Here is what writing sloppy actually communicates to the creative voice:

It tells the two-year-old in the back of the brain that the work does not matter. The critical voice up front will fix everything later. So why should the creative voice bother? It disengages.

The result is familiar to almost every writer who has ever tried to produce a rough draft: the story bogs down. The pages look terrible. The plot stops making sense. Nothing interesting happens. The critical voice declares the entire project worthless, and the book goes into a drawer.

This is not a failure of talent or discipline. It is the predictable consequence of a flawed method. The creative voice requires respect to function. Writing sloppy withholds that respect.

**The practical alternative:** Write each sentence as well as possible in the moment. If a detail needs research, look it up immediately. If a sentence is unclear, cycle back and fix it before moving forward. Do not leave brackets, placeholders, or notes to self. Write it right the first time.

This does not mean writing slowly. The pace of forward motion is not the issue. The issue is intention. A writer who moves quickly while staying clean in the creative voice is doing something entirely different from a writer who types carelessly under the assumption that problems will be corrected later.

NaNoWriMo, in its standard form, promotes writing sloppy. Fifty thousand words of typing is not fifty thousand words of story. The words produced under "just get it down" instructions tend to be inert — they were generated by the critical voice performing a task, not by the creative voice telling a story.

### How Cycling Works — and Why It Exists

**Cycling** emerged naturally when writers transitioned from typewriters to word processors.

On a typewriter, clean writing required extraordinary discipline because every correction was costly — retyping entire pages to fix a single line. Prolific writers of the pulp era trained themselves to write clean on the first pass or not at all. They had no alternative.

Word processors changed everything. The ability to jump back into a manuscript, add a sentence, adjust a paragraph, and then return to the forward edge of the story opened an entirely new way of working. Writers discovered independently, and then compared notes, that they were all doing the same thing: cycling back through recent material before pushing forward.

The method:

1. Write forward for 300–500 words, then bog down.
2. Jump out of the story (stop writing new material).
3. Go back to a point roughly 300–500 words behind the stopping point.
4. Read forward toward the stopping point, making only positive, additive changes.
5. Arrive at the stopping point with momentum and push through it.
6. Write new material for another 300–500 words.
7. Repeat.

Over the course of a novel, cycling means the opening has been read ten or fifteen times, not one. Each pass adds texture, tightens voice, fixes continuity problems before they compound. By the time the last page is written, the first page is as clean as the writer can make it.

Cycling also prevents the "first fifty pages are great, then it fell apart" phenomenon. Because the writer is constantly cycling back, the story remains coherent and the voice remains consistent. There is no seam where the writer changed their approach.

### The Key Trick: Write the Next Sentence

When writing into the dark, the question "what happens next?" is a trap. It is a critical-voice question. It invites planning, which invites the analytical brain into a process that only the creative voice can navigate.

The correct question is much smaller: **what is the next sentence?**

Not the next chapter. Not the next scene. The next sentence. Logically, given the sentence just written, what sentence would follow? Write that one. Then ask the same question again.

This sounds almost too simple to be useful. It is not. The sentence-by-sentence approach keeps the writer in the story, in the creative voice, and in forward motion. It prevents the freezing that occurs when the writer tries to plot the next hundred pages.

The trick is most useful at glitch points — the moments when cycling fails to restore momentum and the writer is genuinely stuck. The fix is to cycle further back than usual (halfway through the material rather than a few hundred words) and, upon reaching the stopping point, ask only: what is the next sentence?

### Warning Signs: Recognizing the Critical Voice

The critical voice does not announce itself. It infiltrates the writing process disguised as reasonable concern. These are the signs it has taken over:

- **Negative judgment:** "This plot is stupid." "This character doesn't work." "I wrote myself into a corner." These thoughts always originate in the critical voice. The creative voice is never negative — it is just playing.
- **The need to figure out what happens next:** Any impulse to stop writing and think through the plot is a critical-voice impulse. This feels responsible but is actually a shutdown mechanism.
- **The feeling that the story is garbage:** Somewhere between pages 50 and 100, almost every novel looks terrible to its author. This is not a truthful assessment of the work — it is the critical voice asserting control at the moment when the opening's excitement has worn off.
- **Planning the next chapter before writing it:** Outlining the next section "just to feel more confident" is the critical voice offering a false safety net. The net shreds the creative voice's engagement.

When any of these warning signs appear, the response is the same: cycle back. Add depth. Write the next sentence. Do not obey the critical voice.

The motto worth posting over the writing desk: **Trust the process.**

### Time Is the Resource

Time is every writer's constraint. The more of it spent on preparation, revision, and planning, the less available for actually producing new fiction.

This is not an argument for rushing or for producing less-than-best-effort work. It is an argument for efficiency. Every minute spent outlining a book that will then be rewritten three times is a minute not spent on a book that doesn't exist yet.

Consider the math: a writer who produces one clean draft per book produces twice as much fiction as a writer who produces two drafts per book, in the same time. Over a career of twenty years, the difference is not ten books versus twenty — it is the difference between a career and a legacy.

Time spent on writing sloppy and then correcting it is also lost time. Writing clean in the first pass is faster overall than writing sloppy and rewriting.

Protect the writing time. Use it to write, not to prepare to write.

### Exercise / Action Items

**Assignment 3, Part 1:** Pick a trigger from these two options: "Cure for the Sleeping Woman" or "Merchant of All Power." Type the title, then begin writing immediately. Do not think about it first. Write 200–300 words, then stop and cycle back to the beginning. Write through to 300–400 words, then cycle again. Write through to 500–700 words, then stop. Three cycles minimum. Submit what you have at that point — no spell-checking, no polishing.

**Assignment 3, Part 2:** Identify a project you are currently stuck on. Answer these five questions about it:

1. Why are you stuck?
2. What will happen if you keep writing despite being stuck?
3. Do you actually like this project, or is it something you feel obligated to write?
4. Why are you writing it?
5. What are you afraid of?

Answer honestly. The fear question is the most important one.

### Key Takeaways

- The ultimate goal is one clean draft, finished, released. No second drafts.
- Writing sloppy disengages the creative voice. Write each sentence as well as possible in the moment.
- Cycling is the technical mechanism that produces clean first drafts. It is not rewriting — it is momentum-building.
- The correct question at any stopping point is "what is the next sentence?" — not "what happens next?"
- Warning signs that the critical voice has taken control: negative thoughts, the need to plan, the feeling that the project is garbage.
- Time spent outlining, rewriting, and planning is time not spent creating new fiction. Protect the writing time.


## Week 4: The Middle — Sticking Points and the Art of Not Caring

Week four addresses the center of the novel — the section between the opening and the climax where writers are most likely to stop entirely. This week covers how the English-teacher method predictably breaks down at the hundred-page mark, what is genuinely different about writing into the dark at that same point, the specific techniques for getting through the middle, how "not caring" functions as a creative strategy, and how difficulty is handled without freezing.

### The English-Teacher Breakdown at Page 100

Every writer trained in the conventional approach — plot first, draft second, revise repeatedly — encounters the same experience at approximately the end of the first act.

The process looks like this:

1. The book is plotted. Excitement is high. The writer begins.
2. The opening comes together reasonably well. Momentum builds.
3. At roughly page 100 — the end of the beginning — the excitement drains away overnight.
4. The critical voice switches from facilitation to evaluation. Everything the writer has produced suddenly looks like garbage.
5. The outline, which was sketchy through the middle section, offers nothing useful.
6. The writer attempts to force themselves through a section they no longer believe in.
7. The book stops.

This is not a personal failure. It is the predictable result of a method that depends on the critical voice for both planning and execution. The critical voice can sustain a performance of enthusiasm while following a plan, but it cannot supply genuine creative energy. When the plan runs out, the pretense collapses.

The critical voice's entire function is to stop the writer. It is patient. It waits for the opening excitement to exhaust itself, and then it takes over. Every trick a writer tries to fight through at this point — outlining the next chapter, jumping ahead to write a later scene, calculating how far away the climax is — uses the critical voice. And the critical voice's goal is to stop. The tricks become the mechanism of stopping.

### What Writing into the Dark Feels at the Same Point

The writer working into the dark hits a similar stopping point at the end of the opening, roughly the same number of pages in. It feels identical: the story stops, the writer does not know what happens next, and discomfort sets in.

The difference is what the discomfort means.

For the writer using a conventional method, not knowing what happens next signals that the plan is inadequate. The critical voice must now intervene to construct a new plan.

For the writer working in the dark, not knowing what happens next is **the correct condition.** It means the story is genuinely open. The reader who eventually picks up this book will have the same experience of not knowing. If the writer doesn't know what happens next, the reader won't either. That is not a problem — it is the goal.

The temptation, at this point, is exactly the same: stop and figure out what happens next. Resist it. That temptation is the critical voice using the same familiar mechanism. The moment the writer obeys — the moment planning begins — the creative voice disengages and the story shifts from discovery to execution of a known outcome. The writing becomes exactly as dull as its predetermined destination.

Celebrate not knowing. It is the signal that the story is alive.

### The Tools for Getting Through

When the stopping point arrives in the middle of a book, three specific tools help:

**1. Cycle further back.**
Instead of cycling back 300–500 words, go halfway back through the manuscript — not all the way to the beginning, but far enough to re-enter the story's energy. Read forward at depth. Let the creative voice re-engage with what already exists before reaching the stopping point. The momentum built through that re-reading is often enough to push through.

**2. Write the next sentence.**
Not the next chapter. Not the next scene. The next sentence. Given the last sentence written, what sentence logically follows? Write that one sentence. Then ask the question again. The creative voice picks up speed through this process and often reaches full momentum within a few pages.

**3. Write the next scene.**
A slightly broader version of the sentence trick: identify where the character currently is and what they are doing. Start a new scene in that location. Add depth — sensory detail, character opinion, physical specificity. Do not worry about what the scene means for the plot. Just write it. The creative voice will connect it.

**A fourth tool, often overlooked:** A ten-minute nap. This is not a joke. When completely stuck at a chapter break, lying down and specifically thinking "I need to know what the next scene is" — not the plot, not what happens next, just the next scene — reliably produces an answer from the creative voice. The answer arrives because the analytical brain has been put to sleep and the creative voice has been given the problem directly.

### Not Caring: The Week's Theme

The theme of week four is **not caring** — a phrase that requires careful definition.

Not caring does not mean indifference to craft or story. It does not mean producing inferior work. It means refusing to let the following concerns enter the writing space:

- What readers will think
- Whether the book will sell
- What a first reader will say when the draft is done
- Whether the premise is commercial
- Whether the story is "important"
- What critics will do with it

All of these concerns have one thing in common: they are external. They belong after the draft is finished. They have no business appearing during the drafting process, because their presence automatically engages the critical voice, which then uses them as reasons to stop.

The only thing that matters while writing a draft is the story. Not caring about everything else is the practice of protecting the story from interference.

This is a line, not a wall. It requires continuous maintenance. External concerns try to slip back in. The writer's job is to notice when they have and to push them back out again, returning attention to the story.

### Difficulty: The Honest Assessment

Writing into the dark is not always easy. Week four is the most difficult week in the workshop to teach because the techniques for the middle of a book cannot be practiced in isolation — they can only be tested inside an actual novel in progress.

The difficulty is real. The training to figure out what happens next is deep. The critical voice is practiced at this particular shutdown mechanism. Every workshop a writer has attended, every book on craft, every advice column — most of them reinforce the habit of planning and plotting, which means the habit of critical-voice control.

Breaking that habit requires noticing it each time it activates and choosing differently. Not once — every time. The choice does not become automatic. It becomes easier with practice, but it remains a choice.

The single question to ask when difficulty arrives: Would a reader who loved this book and had reached this exact stopping point want to flip ahead fifty pages to find out what happens?

No. Doing that would ruin the book for them.

Writing the plot resolution before arriving at it naturally does exactly that to the writing experience. The story is already known. The energy collapses. The only way to preserve the experience — for writer and reader both — is to not know, and to write the next sentence from within that not-knowing.

### Exercise / Action Items

**Assignment 4:** Write an opening into the dark using this trigger: an attractive man or woman, walking down the street, dressed wrong for the context. Cycle through to 500–800 words. Focus on depth first — get into the character's experience of the situation. The "dressed wrong" element creates an out-of-place detail that the character (and the story) must reckon with.

Note: out-of-place details are among the most powerful triggers available to the writer working in the dark. They create immediate questions without requiring a plot, and they pull the creative voice directly into active mode.

### Key Takeaways

- The conventional method's failure at page 100 is predictable and structural — not a failure of individual writers.
- Writing into the dark hits a similar stopping point, but not-knowing is the correct condition, not a signal that something is wrong.
- The tools for the middle: cycle further back, write the next sentence, write the next scene. Ten-minute naps work.
- "Not caring" means refusing to allow external concerns — sales, reviews, readers, importance — into the writing space. They belong after the draft.
- The difficulty of the middle is real. The technique requires noticing the habit of planning and choosing differently, every time.
- The check: would a reader flip ahead to find out what happens? No. Then don't do it as a writer either.


## Week 5: Endings — Structure, Fear, and Finishing

Week five covers the final phase of a novel written into the dark: the approach to the ending, the structure of endings in Western fiction, what the climax is and is not, how validation works, the phenomenon of writing past the ending, and what to do when the book is finished. This is the fear-heaviest week in the workshop, because the approach to the ending is where the loudest critical-voice questions arise.

### Critical-Voice Questions at the End

As a novel moves into its final third, a specific set of questions appears. They feel reasonable. They feel like legitimate concerns a responsible writer should address. They are critical-voice shutdown mechanisms:

- How is this going to end?
- Have I wasted my time?
- Who is the bad guy? (For mysteries and thrillers)
- Did I write myself into a corner?

Every one of these questions has the same structure: they position the writer as someone who does not know enough and must stop forward motion to figure it out. They are the same mechanism as "I'd better figure out what happens next" — just dressed in the specific language of endings.

The answers are:

- You will figure out how it ends when you get there.
- No.
- You will find out when you write there.
- No. Writing into a corner is a critical-voice perception, not a real structural problem.

The creative voice, operating without critical-voice interference, has been tracking the story's entire architecture across all the pages written so far. It knows what the ending needs to be. It cannot be told — it must be written. Getting there requires the same practice that got the story through the middle: trust the process. Write the next sentence.

### Understanding the Seven-Point Structure

Western fiction follows a seven-point structure. Understanding this structure — not as a planning tool, but as background knowledge — gives the creative voice permission to use it. The creative voice already knows the structure from decades of reading. Naming it simply makes it conscious.

The seven points:

1. **Character in a setting** — the opening
2. **Character encounters a problem** — the inciting event
3. **Character tries to solve the problem** — first attempt
4. **Character fails** — first failure
5. **Repeated try-fail cycles** — the body of the novel
6. **The final try-fail cycle** — the climax
7. **Validation** — the ending

Points three and four repeat indefinitely. Every subplot, every secondary character, every additional complication adds try-fail cycles. The creative voice tracks all of them simultaneously. The critical voice cannot — it does not have the capacity. This is why outlining the middle of a novel produces sketchy, thin second acts: the critical voice can gesture at plot points but cannot hold the full complexity of what the creative voice has already assembled.

The climax is point six: the final, highest-stakes try-fail cycle. Every thread converges. The central problem reaches its peak. The outcome is determined.

**The climax is not the ending.** This is one of the most common structural errors in beginning and intermediate fiction. Stopping the story at the climax leaves readers profoundly unsatisfied. The validation is what makes the ending feel like an ending.

### The Validation: What It Is and How to Recognize It

The **validation** is the section of the novel between the climax and the last word. Its purpose: to tell the reader, clearly and without ambiguity, that the story is over.

Several consistent markers identify the validation:

- **A time jump.** After the climax ends and a chapter break occurs, the next chapter begins some amount of time later — hours, days, weeks, or months. This time jump is a near-universal feature of Western fiction's structure. It signals the transition from action to resolution.
- **Rich, thick depth.** The prose slows down and becomes more detailed — more like the opening than like the accelerated pacing of the climax. Characters are described again in their settings. Opinions and emotions reappear.
- **Movement stops.** People are sitting, talking, having a meal, attending a ceremony. The physical action of the plot has concluded. Characters are processing.
- **Plot threads close.** Minor characters are accounted for. Loose ends are tied. Questions raised earlier in the novel find their answers, often in dialogue.

When writing into the dark, the validation often writes itself. The creative voice knows these elements need to exist and produces them when given forward momentum. The time jump, the slowing of pace, the gathering of characters — these emerge naturally if the writer does not stop at the climax.

The risk is stopping at the climax because it feels like a natural ending point. It is not. Readers who reach the last try-fail cycle are not done. They need the validation. Without it, the book feels abrupt and unresolved, even if the plot question was answered.

### Writing Past Your Ending

One of the most common experiences when writing into the dark: the writer gets through the validation, the prose begins to slow, and then the story just stops. The writer bogs down exactly as they did in the middle.

This is not the same as the middle-of-book stopping point. This is the creative voice signaling that the ending has already been written — and the writer missed it.

When this happens in the validation:

1. Do not try to write forward.
2. Come out of the story's timeline entirely.
3. Cycle back to the start of the validation — where the time jump occurred.
4. Read forward through the validation, looking specifically for the moment the book feels complete.
5. Usually, the actual ending is 300–500 words before where the writer stopped.
6. Add a line or two to complete the thought, cut what follows, and release.

The creative voice shuts down after the ending passes because there is nothing more to say. Continuing to write past that point does not produce more story — it produces text the reader will experience as the book going on longer than it should.

The bog-down in the validation is the creative voice saying: we are done here. Listen to it.

### After the Draft: Release

When the ending is found and the manuscript is complete, the question of what to do next has a clear answer: release it.

This means:

1. Cycle through the validation once more, since it is the section cycled through least during drafting.
2. Cycle through the climax once, for the same reason.
3. Spell-check.
4. Send to a copy editor if one is available. (Not a developmental editor. A copy editor who finds typos and continuity errors, not one who restructures the story.)
5. Format and publish.
6. Begin the next book.

The writer does not look at the manuscript again after release. It was written clean. It was cycled. It is done. Reading it again would be rereading a book already read. The time is better spent elsewhere.

The first reader's role is limited: find typos and continuity errors (broken legs that heal mid-chapter; characters with contradictory eye colors). The story itself is not in question. Trust it.

### Studying Endings: How to Give the Creative Voice Permission

The creative voice already knows how to write endings. It has been reading them for years. The problem is not knowledge — it is permission.

Studying endings explicitly — by analyzing the validation sections of novels by accomplished writers — gives the creative voice conscious awareness of what it already knows implicitly. That awareness is permission. The two-year-old can now say: I've seen this technique in action and I'm allowed to use it.

The study method: take three contemporary novels by accomplished writers in any genre. Find each book's validation section. Analyze:

- How long is it (approximate word count)?
- What techniques does the author use to signal that the story is over?
- Does the validation refer back to anything from the opening?
- Why does this ending work emotionally?

Do not read critically. Read curiously. The goal is not to dissect but to notice. Every technique noticed expands the creative voice's toolkit for the next project.

### Exercise / Action Items

**Assignment 5:** Choose three contemporary novels by accomplished writers. For each one:

1. Identify the validation section and estimate its length.
2. Describe the techniques the author uses in the validation.
3. Note whether anything in the validation refers back to the opening.
4. Write two or three paragraphs on why this ending works for you as a reader.

Do not blow this assignment off. It is the most difficult in the workshop and among the most valuable. The self-consciousness of looking at endings — really looking, paragraph by paragraph — gives the creative voice more tools than any amount of instruction.

### Key Takeaways

- Critical-voice questions at the end of a novel are shutdown mechanisms. The answers to all of them: you will figure it out when you write there.
- The climax is not the ending. It is point six of seven. The validation — the section after the climax — is the actual ending.
- The validation is marked by a time jump, thick depth, stopped movement, and closing of plot threads.
- Writing past the ending is common. If the writer bogs down in the validation, cycle back to find where the ending actually is.
- After the draft: cycle the validation and climax once, spell-check, release. Do not reread. Write the next book.
- Studying endings by accomplished writers gives the creative voice explicit permission to use techniques it already knows.


## Week 6: Protecting the Work — Fun, Fear, and Importance

Week six is the validation of this workshop — the section where the central principles are reviewed, integrated, and sharpened. The week's primary focus is on the word "important" and what happens to the creative process when a book, a project, or a writing session becomes weighted with significance. The week also addresses fear, how to redirect it, and how to summarize the four core practices that carry the writer from first word to finished book.

### The Most Deadly Word in Fiction Writing

The most destructive force in writing into the dark is not a skill deficit. It is a word: **important.**

When a book becomes important, the critical voice has been given full permission to operate. Important opens every door the critical voice was previously locked out of. Important is the key to the room where the critical voice lives.

What makes a book feel important? Any external pressure attached to the outcome:

- An editor is waiting for it.
- A challenge group is reading it as it progresses.
- Friends and family have been told about it.
- The writer has publicly committed to a deadline.
- The book is in a series that readers are already invested in.
- The writer believes this is the project that will change their career.

All of these create external stakes. External stakes give the critical voice its most powerful argument: this matters, therefore you must be careful, therefore you must stop and think, therefore you must not risk what you have already written on anything you cannot control.

The creative voice cannot work under this kind of pressure. The two-year-old does not perform on demand. It plays. It explores. Telling a two-year-old that their play is important — that the outcome matters, that others are watching — does not inspire better play. It freezes the child.

When you notice a book becoming important, the immediate counter-practice is to write for yourself. For yourself as the reader of this story, not as the producer of an important cultural product. No one else is in the room. The book is for you. Write it for you.

### Where "Important" Shows Up

The critical voice inserts importance at predictable points:

**At the opening.** Writers spend enormous energy trying to craft a perfect opening because they have heard, correctly, that openings matter to readers and publishers. The opening becomes important. Every word of it becomes precious. The writer cycles endlessly in the first page, unable to move forward because forward motion will take the story somewhere imperfect.

The antidote: the opening is just a beginning. It needs a character in a setting. Write it and keep moving. The cover and the blurb reach readers before the opening does anyway.

**At individual words and sentences.** When time is constrained, each writing session feels like it must produce only perfect material. The fifteen minutes available must not be wasted on wrong words. This makes the words important, which makes the critical voice watch every word, which makes forward motion impossible.

The antidote: what matters is sitting down and writing, not that every word produced is optimal. The practice of writing is what is important, not the results of any individual session.

**In the middle of the book.** Knowing what happens next becomes important. The critical voice frames not knowing as a dangerous gap that must be filled before forward motion is safe.

The antidote: do not know what happens next. Write the next sentence.

**At the ending.** Knowing the ending becomes important. Writers convince themselves they cannot proceed without knowing where the story is going.

The antidote: knowing the ending removes the writer's interest in writing it. If the ending is already known, the story is already over. Discovery requires unknowing.

### Fear: The Critical Voice's Core Tool

Fear underlies all of it. Fear of wasting time. Fear of failure. Fear of bad reviews. Fear of readers not buying the book. Fear of the ending not working. Fear that the book is garbage.

Fear is the critical voice operating at full capacity. Every fear thought about the writing is a critical-voice thought. Every one.

Fear is not useful information. It is a protective mechanism that evolved to keep humans alive in physically dangerous environments. Writing is not a physically dangerous environment. Nothing bad can happen in the act of putting words on a page. The critical voice's fear response is misfiring.

The one fear worth cultivating: **the fear of not writing.**

Not writing is genuinely dangerous — to the career, to the creative capacity, to the long-term relationship with storytelling. Writers who stop writing stop being writers. That is worth fearing. Redirect the fear: when the critical voice generates anxiety about the current project, consciously shift the fear to the question of not writing at all. The critical voice's energy then serves the work instead of stopping it.

When fear of a specific project surfaces, ask: what am I afraid of losing if I write this the way the creative voice wants to? The answer is usually reputation, safety, certainty. None of those can be protected by not writing. Writing the next sentence and accepting uncertainty is always the correct response.

### What Is Actually Important

Only one thing is worth the weight of importance in the writing process: **the experience of having fun.**

This is not a consolation prize. It is the mechanism. Writers who are genuinely enjoying the process produce books that are genuinely enjoyable to read. The feeling comes through every word, every syntax choice, every structural decision the creative voice makes. Readers feel the writer's engagement just as surely as they feel a writer's dread.

Making the enjoyment important — protecting it, prioritizing it, returning to it when the critical voice has taken over — is the practice. Not making the outcome important. Not making the quality important. Not making the reception important. The enjoyment of writing this story, today, for yourself.

When writing becomes the place where you go to have fun — genuinely, not as an affirmation — it begins to perform a secondary function: it provides relief from external stress. The problems of the real world are not present in the writing session. There is just a character, a setting, and the next sentence. That isolation, protected and maintained, becomes increasingly valuable.

### The Four Core Practices

The final summary of writing into the dark reduces to four practices:

**1. Write clean.**
Every sentence, as written. No placeholders. No brackets for research to be done later. If a detail requires research, look it up immediately, then return and write it. A clean draft is the goal, and cleanliness is built sentence by sentence, not imposed afterward.

**2. Cycle.**
Constantly. The word processor makes this possible. Go back, add, strengthen, touch — always positively — and then push through the stopping point. The story is built from cycling. The result is a manuscript that has been read ten or fifteen times before the last page is written, and that is clean and complete when the ending is found.

**3. Don't know where you're going.**
Make it a point of practice. Do not outline. Do not plan the ending. Do not figure out who the bad guy is before writing there. When the critical voice demands a map, refuse. If a question must be asked, the question is only: what is the next sentence?

**4. Have fun.**
Write for yourself. Write the stories you want to read. Write in the genres and with the characters that genuinely excite you. If a project feels like an obligation, examine whether it is worth pursuing. If a session is drudgery, something has gone wrong and needs to be identified and fixed.

These four practices, applied consistently across a novel — and across a career — are what the workshop has been building toward.

### Release and Move On

When the book is done, release it.

Get it formatted. Get it to readers through every available channel. Then begin the next book.

The writer who finishes and immediately starts the next project is building the thing that makes a career: a body of work. Readers come back for the next book. Catalog value builds over time. Each new release helps everything already published.

The writer who finishes and then spends months revising, second-guessing, and seeking validation from first readers, critique groups, and beta readers is stalled. That time is lost. The next book does not exist yet. The current book is probably not being improved — it is being interfered with.

Trust what was written. It was written clean, cycled, and produced by the creative voice in full engagement. Release it. Write the next one.

- Identify which belief systems were most challenged.
- Identify which techniques produced the clearest results in the assignments.
- Identify the specific fears and critical-voice patterns that were most active in your own writing.
- Apply everything to the current project, or to starting the next one.

### Key Takeaways

- The word "important" opens every door the critical voice uses to stop the writer. When a book becomes important, write for yourself only.
- Important shows up at openings, individual sentences, the middle of books, and at the ending. The antidote is always the same: write the next sentence.
- Fear is the critical voice's core mechanism. The only fear worth cultivating is the fear of not writing.
- What is actually important: the enjoyment of writing. Protect it.
- The four practices: write clean, cycle, don't know where you're going, have fun.
- When the book is done, release it. Write the next one. Build the catalog. Trust the work.
